Mind Mapping

Who is this for?

  • This is for anyone who is trying to create new ideas or tie ideas together in a different way
  • It can be for someone who wants to take notes in a new way

Works best when...

Maybe not for you IF...

Who's involved? How long?

  • Really a singular activity, but can be facilitated in any size group
  • Timebox yourself, name your time

Materials you will need

  • Unlined paper
  • Pencils (pens are harder)
  • Colored pencils, if desired
  • A timer
  • A topic you want to explore

Why mindmapping?

  • If you want to find a way to better remember the notes that are taken
  • Understand something on a deeper level than you do right now
  • It can link together disparate ideas in unique ways

How does it work?

Really, there are just a few simple rules, and then some best practices that can help you later on.

  • Start with a central idea
  • Add branches to your map, or start writing ideas, grouping similar ideas togther and your branches will come together logically
  • Add keywords to your branches
  • Color code your branches
  • Include images whenever possible and appropriate, they can convey so much more meaning than the written word.

Some best practices might include the following:

  • Place the sheet of paper horizontally in front of you
  • Draw a reasonably-sized memorable central image that represents what you are mapping out
  • Use colors throughout, it draws in a different part of your brain
  • Use one key word per line (or branch)
  • Keep it organized, there's nothing wrong with redrawing a map if you find a part is becoming too cluttered
  • Use emphasis to show associations in your mindmap
  • Images can represent words in a metaphorical, sound-like, or in a direct manner
  • You can create boundaries and borders to highlight certain areas of hte map.
  • You can use different font styles to separate ideas and topics
  • Branch endings can be varried to emphasize words or phrases
  • Vary image colors, emphasis, and size to enhance memory and recall

Pitfalls to avoid

  • Self-editing is one of the worst things you can do as you perform these creative processes. Use pens, and if you feel something really doesn't belong just put a single line through it.
  • Don't feel like your first draft will be your only draft. It may help to redraw them as your understanding of a piece expands.
  • Don't be afraid to use a "parking lot" section for pieces where you aren't sure where they belong yet.
  • Using computer software exclusively. There is something in drawing/writing out ideas for most people that helps cement the ideas in our heads.

Helpful to understand

  • Visual maps don't require the use of images to effectively convey information. They can be built around key words and phrases in a similar fashion.
