Value proposition panvas

What is it?

When looking at a value proposition canvas, we are looking at a subset of a typical business model. We are looking specifically at a specific persona, as well as what products or services we offer to fit the need of that customer. The goal of this tool is to help provide a good fit between the products and services that we offer (or potentially to identify what gaps exist in those products/services) and the personas we are targeting. When you match the products/services to the peronas' needs, that is called a product-market fit (you match your products to the market demands).

Each of the boxes should be force ranked with the most important piece at the top of the box.\

It is simply an overview of what the persona wants and needs, and why the customer feels that way. It can be a great first step, actual implementation of that product is a multi-disciplinary effort between UI/UX experts, architects, engineers, marketing, etc.

The persona (on the right)

Persona jobs

In the persona jobs box, you gather the needs of the persona, the problems that they are trying to solve, and the tasks that they are tryigng to perform or complete. Remember that these tasks can be direct tasks (tasks that they are trying to do right now), indirect tasks (somethign they expect to happen as a result of their direct tasks), or emotional tasks (feeling good about using the product/service). An immediate task could be uploading an existing website. An indirect task could then be something like driving more customers to their store because of a website. An emotional task coudl be feeling successful completing the jobs at hand. It is also good to look at wht social jobs the persona is tryign to achieve, like looking good in the surrounding community.

Some questions to ask yourself when you are working on this part of VPC:

  • What functional jobs is the persona tryting to get done? (perform a specific task, solve a specific problem, etc)
  • What social jobs is the persona trying to get done? (trying to look good, gain power, gain status, etc.)
  • What emotional jobs is the persona trying to accomplish? (aesthetics, feel good, security, etc.)
  • What basic needs is your persona trying to satisfy? (communication, etc.)

Persona pain points

In this box, you will gather all of the negative emotions or undesired costs, situations, or risks that a persona could experiende before, during, or after getting their job done. You should include any frustrations the persona could experience during this process. Think about what costs are associated with the persona that they may find undesirable, what negative situations does this process put the persona through, and what risks does the persona potentially experience during this process.

Some questions to ask yourself when you are working on this part of the VPC:

  • What does your persona find too costly? (too much time, too much money, requires substantial efforts, etc.)
  • What makes your persona feel bad? (frustrations, annoyances, things that give them a headache, etc.)
  • How are current solutions underperforming for your persona? (lack of features, performance, malfunctioning, etc.)
  • What are the main difficulties and challenges your persona encounters? (understanding how things work, difficulties getting things done, resistance, etc.)
  • What negative social consequences has your persona encountered so far? (loss of face, power, trust, status, etc.)
  • What risks does your persona fear? (financial, social, technical risks, what could go wrong)
  • What keeps your persona awake at night? (big issues, concerns, worries, etc.)
  • What common mistakes does your persona make? (usage mistakes, etc.)
  • What barriers are keeping your persona from adopting solutions? (upfront investment costs, learning curve, resistance to change, etc.)

Persona gains

In the persona gains box, you record all the beneifts and desires of a persona. You also record personal, functional, or economic gains the persona is looking forward to. Make sure that you include positive emotions, functional requirements, and/or cost savings the persona wants to receive. Antoehr thing to look for is wha twill the persona be positively surprised by.

Some questions to ask yourself when you are working on this part of the VPC:

  • Which savings would make your persona happy? (in terms of time, money, effort, etc.)
  • What outcomes does your persona expect and what would go beyond his/her expectations? (quality level, more of something, less of something, etc.)
  • How do current solutions delight your persona? (specific features, performance, quality)
  • What would make your persona's job or life easier? (flatter learning curve, more services, lower cost of ownership, etc.)
  • What positive consequences does your persona desire? (make them look good, increase in power, status, etc.)
  • What is your persona looking for? (good design, guarantees, specific or more features, etc.)
  • What does your persona dream about? (big achievements, big reliefs, etc.)
  • How does your persona measure success and failure? (performance, cost, etc.)
  • What would incrase the likelihood of adopting a solution? (lower cost, less investments, lower risk, better quality, performance, design, etc.)

Our products and services

Products and services

Here is where you list the products and services that we offer that this value proposition is built around. Things to keep in mind are the functional, social, or emotional status of hte persona. This is what teh persona is going to use to solve his/her problems.

  • Are the products or services crucial or trivial to achieving the persona's goals?

Pain relievers

In this box, you describe how the products and services that you list can help alleviate teh challenges, needs, and pains of the customer. You need to describe how these products and services eliminate negative emotions, undesired costs, or avoidable situations. You should look at how frequently this happens for our persona as we rank these potential solutions.

Ask if the products or services fill any of the following needs:

  • Do they produce savings? (time, money, effort, etc.)
  • Do they make the persona feel better? (kill frustrations, annoyances, things that give them a headache, etc.)
  • Do they fix underperforming solutions? (new features, better performance, better quality, etc.)
  • Do they put an end to difficulties and challenges your persona encounters? (make things easier, help them get things done, eliminate resistance, etc.)
  • Do they wip out negative social consequences your persona encounters or fears? (loss of face, power, trust, or status)
  • Do they eliminate risks your persona fears? (technical, social, financial, etc.)
  • Do they help the customer sleep better at night? (help with big issues, diminish concerns, eliminate worries, etc.)
  • Do they limit or eradicate common mistakes your persona could make? (usage mistakes, etc.)
  • Do they get rid of barriers that are keeping your persona from adopting solutions? (lower or no upfront investment costs, flatter learning curve, less resistance to change, etc.)

Gain creators

Gain creators describe how the products or services add value to the customer. When looking at these, we should ask the following questions:

  • Do they create savings that make your persona happy? (time, money, effort, etc.)
  • Do they produce the outcomes your persona expects or that go beyond their expectations? (better quality level, more of something, less of something, etc.)
  • Do they copy or out perform current solutions that delight your persona? (regarding specific features, performance, quality, etc.)
  • Do they make your persona's life easier? (flatter learning curve, usability, accessibility, more services, lower cost of ownership, etc.)
  • Do they create positive social consequences that your persona desires? (makes them look good, produces an increase in power, status, money, etc.)
  • Do they do something the persona is looking for? (good design, guarantees, specific or more features, etc.)
  • Do tehy produce positive outcomes matching your persona's success and failure criteria? (better performance, lower cost, etc.)
  • Do they help make adoption easier? (lower cost, less investments, lower risk, better quality, performance, design, etc.)

Remember, there are multiple products out there for customers

For each persona you are looking at, it would be a good idea to perform this same analysis on competing products. This way, you can identify ways to modify a product to better meet the needs of that persona. If creating a new product, it would be a great idea to do this analysis first to see how the product could be marketed, designed, and created to fill in where other products lack.
